Abstract
This diploma project presents the development of an application for AI-enhanced gamebased learning. The study addresses a persistent limitation of many digital learning tools:
they often provide the same tasks, the same pace, and the same feedback for all learners,
which can reduce engagement and overlook differences in prior knowledge, progress, and
motivation.
The thesis combines a literature review on gamification, adaptive learning, intelligent tutoring systems, learning analytics, and responsible AI in education with an
updated mixed-methods survey analysis based on 100 responses. The results show that
students value immediate feedback, retries, visible progress, and meaningful challenge
more consistently than purely speed-based competition.
On the basis of these findings, the project proposes a minimum viable product
and a service-oriented architecture for a mobile-first educational platform. The system
integrates learner modelling, game-based practice, AI-assisted hints and explanations,
teacher analytics, and controlled safety mechanisms. The selected technology stack
combines Flutter, FastAPI, Firebase, S3/MinIO, and an LLM-based tutoring workflow
orchestrated through n8n. In practical terms, the project offers a research-grounded
and technically feasible blueprint for a personalized, engaging, and scalable learning
application.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Rapid digitalization of education has opened new possibilities for interactive and personalized learning, but it has also raised some nagging problems that traditional forms of
instruction and many standard e-learning tools are not entirely capable of remedying.
In a lot of educational spaces, students are still faced with repetitive exercises, delayed
feedback, and one-size-fits-all learning sequences that do not respond well to differences
in prior knowledge, pace, motivation, or learning preferences. As a result, even digital
platforms can recreate the same disengagement that is evident in conventional instruction.
This challenge makes it necessary to transition from static content delivery to systems
that are able to simultaneously motivate learners and also adapt to individual progress
(Dichev & Dicheva, 2017; Li & Shi, 2023; Cuellar-Rojas & Guemes-Castorena, 2022).
One of the most influential answers to this challenge has been the use of gamification
and games-based learning. Although these concepts are related, they are not the same
thing. Gamification is the incorporation of game elements such as points, levels, badges,
leaderboards and progress bars, and rewards into non-game learning experiences, while
game-based learning is where game activity is used as the primary mode of learning. The
distinction is important for this thesis as the topic is not merely about adding decorative
mechanics to an existing platform. Rather, it has to do with the development of an
application where the learning process is organized through game-based interaction and
augmented with artificial intelligence so that the experience is adaptive, data-informed,
and pedagogically purposeful (Dichev & Dicheva, 2017; Huang & Soman, 2013; Chiotaki
& Karpouzis, 2023).
The literature gives strong evidence that well designed gameful learning environments
can improve motivation, engagement and in the right conditions learning outcomes.
Critical reviews and meta-analyses indicate that these effects are real but are quite

sensitive to the quality of the design, the duration of the learning, the learning context
and the extent to which game mechanics are aligned with educational goals. In other
words, while game elements are effective to the extent that they support retrieval practice,
feedback, mastery progression, and meaningful challenge, to the extent that they function
as superficial rewards that are not tied to the learning task itself, game elements are
much less effective (Dichev & Dicheva, 2017; Li & Shi, 2023; Gui & Tai, 2023).
Studies of popular classroom software like Kahoot! and Quizizz can help to understand
the promise and limitations of current game-based educational tools. Research indicates
that such platforms often add more engagement, fun and focus to the classroom through
the introduction of instant feedback, competition and visible progress and the ability to
repeat short tasks. At the same time, these tools are generally poor in their adaptivity.
They tend to show identical questions/activity structures to everyone, depend on their
fixed difficulty and offer limited personalization, beyond pacing or displaying scores. This
can mean that they can be useful as baseline examples, but they are not yet the full
potential of AI driven game-based learning applications (Aibar-Almazan and GonzalezMartin, 2024; Bicen and Kocakoyun, 2018; Razali and Salleh, 2020; Smiderle and de
Oliveira, 2020; Qureshi and Khatoon, 2023).
This is the one limitation where artificial intelligence comes in handy. Research on
adaptive game-based learning and Artificial Intelligence (AI)-enabled adaptive learning
systems reveals that there are several levels at which personalization can be applied:
content-selection, difficulty-adjusting, pacing, hint generation, navigation-paths, learner
modeling, and the next-action-recommendation. Instead of treating all users the same way,
an application powered by AI can analyse data based on the interaction, and adapt the
experience based on what the learner needs at that moment. Such a design is particularly
useful in educational games where the balancing act between challenge and skill has
a great effect on engagement and persistence (Chiotaki & Karpouzis, 2023; Kabudi &
Olsen, 2021).
From a software and system design point of view this adaptability ability requires
more than a game interface. It requires a structured architecture where pedagogical logic,
learner data, game mechanics and AI services work together. Research on intelligent
tutoring systems is relevant in this context because it suggests important elements that
can provide insight into designing the application: a learner model, domain or content
model, a pedagogical model that identifies instructional actions, and an interaction
layer at which the learner experiences the system. Work on gamified intelligent tutoring
systems is further showing that game mechanics and adaptive tutoring should not be

conceived separately but need to be conceptually integrated in order to have consistent
reward systems, progression, and feedback in relation to the educational model (Dermeval
& Vassileva, 2019; Cuellar-Rojas & Guemes-Castorena, 2022). Another great benefit
of the AI-enhanced game-based applications is the potential for embedded analytics
and stealth assessment. In such systems, evidence of learning can be deduced from
gameplay behaviors, response patterns, choices, errors, time on task, or strategy use
without interrupting the flow of play. This enables formative assessment to be supported
in a way that is natural to learners as well as providing richer data to teachers or system
administrators about progress. Recent efforts in learning analytics of serious games and
on game-based assessment have demonstrated that traces from interactions can be used
to support not only the measurement of performance, but also other improvements in
application, personalization, and more timely instructional feedback (Daoudi, 2022; Fang
& McNamara, 2023; Zhu & Yang, 2023; Calvo-Morata & Fernandez-Manjon, 2025).
However, the literature also makes clear that the creation of an effective educational
application in this domain is not only a technical problem. It is just as much a design
problem as it is a user experience problem. User-centered design is still important as
students and teachers do not interact with educational technologies in the same way.
There are differences in competitiveness, motivation, tolerance for public comparison,
and preferred modes of interaction for learners. In addition, poor usability can ruin
even a theoretically sound pedagogical model. Recent studies on educational game
design and development therefore highlight effective products that not only address
game mechanics, but also center on instructional aims, interaction design, analytics,
accessibility and iterative evaluation (Rodrigues & Levrini, 2022; Smiderle & de Oliveira,
2020; Calvo-Morata & Fernandez-Manjon, 2025; Maxim & Arnedo-Moreno, 2025).
There are also some important risks and constraints to be addressed during development. The novelty effect may lead to excitement in the early stages that disappears
if the application doesn’t evolve over time. Too much emphasis on points or rankings
will discourage some learners rather than motivate them. Data-driven personalization
raises issues of transparency, fairness, privacy and human oversight, particularly when
AI is part of the equation for adapting or creating feedback. International guidance on
AI in education emphasise that in education systems have to be held accountable, age
appropriate and subject to ethics, data use with strong limits on learner safety. (Dichev
& Dicheva, 2017; Rodrigues & Levrini, 2022; UNESCO, 2023).
Taken together, these studies reveal an important research and development gap. A
large body of literature evaluates particular tools, particular interventions, or particular

effects of gamification and digital educational games. Another body of literature studies
adaptive learning, intelligent tutoring systems, learning analytics, and AI in education.
Yet fewer works translate these strands into the concrete development of an integrated
application that combines game-based learning, adaptive intelligence, embedded analytics,
and user-centered product design within one coherent software artifact. This gap provides
a strong rationale for the present thesis. The project is therefore positioned not only
as an educational technology study, but also as a software engineering task: to design
and develop an application whose features are directly grounded in the best available
research on AI-enhanced game-based learning (Chiotaki & Karpouzis, 2023; Kabudi &
Olsen, 2021; Dermeval & Vassileva, 2019; Cuéllar-Rojas & Güemes-Castorena, 2022;
Calvo-Morata & Fernández-Manjón, 2025; Maxim & Arnedo-Moreno, 2025).

1.1

Research Aim

The aim of this thesis is to design and develop a prototype application for AI-enhanced
game-based learning that combines game-based interaction, adaptive support, personalized feedback, and learning analytics in a single educational platform.

1.2

Research Objectives

To achieve this aim, the thesis addresses the following objectives:
• To identify the pedagogical, technical, and ethical requirements of an AI-enhanced
game-based learning application on the basis of current academic literature.
• To design the system architecture, user roles, interface logic, and core workflows of the
application, including student interaction, adaptive task flow, feedback mechanisms,
and teacher-facing analytics.
• To implement a functional prototype that includes essential features such as user
profiles, game-based learning activities, progress tracking, adaptive or AI-supported
feedback, and configurable motivational mechanics.
• To conduct a preliminary evaluation of the prototype in terms of usability, perceived
engagement, and the educational relevance of its AI-enhanced features, using an
appropriate validation approach such as expert review, pilot testing, or structured
user feedback.

1.3

Summary

In conclusion, the topic “Development of an Application for AI-Enhanced Game-Based
Learning” is academically and practically justified by the convergence of several established
research directions: gamification, digital game-based learning, adaptive educational
systems, intelligent tutoring, learning analytics, and responsible AI in education. Existing
studies show that game-based and gamified environments can improve learner engagement
and, when properly designed, support stronger learning processes. At the same time,
the literature demonstrates that these benefits are not automatic; they depend on
personalization, sound pedagogy, meaningful feedback, careful UX design, and ethical
use of learner data. For this reason, the proposed thesis does not treat gamification
as a superficial motivational layer. Instead, it approaches application development as
the creation of an integrated educational system in which AI functions as a support
layer for adaptation, analytics, and feedback, while game-based interaction provides
the experiential structure through which learning becomes more active, motivating,
and responsive to individual learner needs. This makes the project relevant both as a
diploma application in software development and as a research-grounded contribution to
contemporary educational technology.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1

Executive Summary

Recent literature on gamification and game-based learning continually reports increases
in learner motivation and engagement with games when game mechanics such as points,
leaderboards, badges, and immediate feedback are utilized. However, the impact on
measurable learning outcomes is variable and it depends on the educational context, the
intervention design and its duration. Critical reviews underline the fact that the empirical foundation on long-term impacts is presently heterogeneous and methodologically
constrained, and thus "surface-level" gamification may create short-term enthusiasm
without creating sustained gains in competence. (Dichev & Dicheva, 2017)
Meta-analyses and systematic reviews provide further clarification of the profile of
these effects. One large meta-analysis focusing on learning outcomes showed a significant
average effect, but with significant moderators, including the type of user, the subject
area, design principles, the duration and the learning environment. (Li, Ma, & Shi,
2023) A meta-analysis on intrinsic motivation found a statistically significant, but small,
overall effect, as well as greater effects on autonomy and relatedness and very little
impact on competence. (Li, Hew, & Du, 2024) With regard to academic achievement, a
meta-analysis of 22 experimental studies of gamification conducted between 2008 and
2023 found the effect of gamification to be moderately positive (Hedges’s g approximately
0.782). (Zeng et al., 2024)
Shifting to the thesis topic of AI enhanced game-based learning, means to jump
beyond gamification as a simple interface layer to rather a model that is centered around
data and adaptivity. AI enhanced systems utilize interaction traces and telemetry to
individualize learning path and content, but a systematic mapping study indicates that
6

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

the number of truly implemented AI-adaptive systems is quite small relative to the
size of the research field. The literature thus calls for the design-oriented studies to
better connect algorithms, user experience and pedagogical goals in a meaningful way.
(Kabudi et al., 2021) In AI-enhanced educational games specifically, a systematic review
of adaptive game-based learning emphasizes the advantages of utilizing performance
and engagement information in the adaptation process during the intervention, but also
observes that adaptation is usually confined to a preset set of content, which limits the
extent of personalization. (Chiotaki et al., 2023)
For the proposed diploma application, the most promising architecture is one in
which five layers of core components are integrated together: (1) a practice engine and
game modes, (2) event collection and teacher analytics dashboards, (3) an AI adaptation
module based on learner profile, progress, and recommendations, (4) built-in stealth
assessment based on gameplay traces, and finally, (5) ethics, safety, and privacy safeguards.
In terms of stealth assessment, deep learning models have been proven to reduce the load
of manual feature engineering and enhance the accuracy and timeliness of competence
prediction, even though it has been shown that model interpretability is a major concern.
(Min et al., 2019/2020) From the point of view of the UX, the literature also indicates that
there is a lack of robust user centered methods for designing digital educational games
and suggests structured approaches to co-design and iterative UX research. (Maxim
& Arnedo-Moreno, 2025) Finally, international advice on generative AI in education
emphasizes the dangers posed by the regulatory void, threats to data privacy, and a
human-centered and age-appropriate approach to ethical validation and pedagogical
design. (UNESCO, 2023)

2.2

Introduction and Scope of the Review

This review is based on an analysis previously prepared for an interactive educational
platform with gamified modes for teachers and students, where the effects of gamification,
the risks associated with novelty, the differences in the platform modes and the architecture
requirements were systematized. Within the present thesis that is narrowed down and
specific: the object of study is an application for AI-enhanced game-based learning, where
gamification is not an end in itself but rather an interface and behavioral layer to support
(a) personalization, (b) adaptive practice, (c) embedded assessment, and (d) analytics
for pedagogical decision-making.
In this review gamification is defined as the integration of game elements into an
7

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

educational context in an effort to improve engagement. A critical review of the ongoing
expansion of the interest in this approach in the context of evidence that is still mixed
with regard to durable learning effects. (Dichev & Dicheva, 2017) At least one line of
empirical research has shown that even such simple elements as points can modify learner
behaviour in the testing situation by modifying response speed and subjective appeal,
though they do not necessarily increase accuracy. (Attali & Arieli-Attali, 2015)
Within the current review, AI enhancement has the meaning of using learningdata analysis algorithms, varying from statistical modeling to machine learning, for
the following four goals: (1) adapting the content, difficulty, and hints; (2) predicting
progress and finding difficulty in learning; (3) automating and enhancing assessment,
including the stealth assessment of assessment; and (4) helping teachers by dashboards
and explanations. This kind of approach falls into the larger category of AI-based adaptive
learning systems that can deliver content based on the individual learner needs. At the
same time, the systematic mapping study suggests that the number of implemented
solutions is still relatively small compared to the size of the research field. (Kabudi et al.,
2021)
The review is delimited into five interrelated areas: (a) empirical effects of gamification
and game-based platforms in particular popular quiz platforms; (b) design frameworks
and factors that influence effectiveness; (c) AI-based adaptivity and personalization in
game-based learning; (d) assessment and analytics and user experience approaches; and
(e) ethics and privacy. Priority is given to primary and/or official sources from the list of
20 works used in this review (15 original sources and 5 more open access sources).

2.3

Gamification and Game Pedagogy Design:
Theories and Design Frameworks

A practice-oriented strand of literature approaches gamification as the design of activity
and feedback loops where game mechanics must be related to learning objectives instead
of acting as decorative features of the interface. The famous five-step design approach goals and constraints, learner profiling, activity and feedback loops, selection of mechanics,
and data-driven iteration - offers an engineering logic for development one close to product
design. (Huang & Soman, 2013) Conceptual work on the gamification of educational
computer games provides a framework for relating gamification principles, system design
aspects and engagement measures, and thus provides useful vocabulary for design and
evaluation purposes. (Nah et al., 2013)
8

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

Critical reviews of empirical studies highlight that, although there is a general tendency
to show that motivation and engagement levels are rising, there are still considerable
mysteries to be aware of: very few comparative studies have been conducted for long
enough; the findings are highly variable; and there is a tendency that the enthusiasm of
practitioners outpaces their knowledge of how it works. (Dichev & Dicheva, 2017) This
leads to an important methodological implication for the thesis: the application should
provide for measurability and verifiability. In other words, it should feature not only
game components but also procedures of gathering data, comparison mechanisms such
as A/B testing or quasi-experiments, transparent rules of rewards, and a clear match
between game actions and education goals.
Another major issue of design is the issue of assessment. Experiments on the use of
points in computer-based testing indicate that gamified incentives can increase response
speed and may initially make the test more attractive, but do not necessarily increase
accuracy and the effect can change from one testing session to the next, which indicates
a possible novelty effect. (Attali & Arieli-Attali, 2015) For designing, this means that
reward mechanics in an educational game need to be aligned to signs of mastery rather
than just activity frequency or speed (otherwise the motivation may be to "faster" or
"more" rather than "more accurate" or "deeper" learning.) (Attali & Arieli-Attali, 2015)

2.4

Gamification Effects on Motivation and Learning
Outcomes

The general empirical evidence backs the claim that gamification does have positive
effects, but only under important conditions. A meta-analysis of learning outcomes from
41 studies, 49 independent samples, and over 5,071 participants demonstrated an overall
effect size that is strong and statistically significant, but also revealed that the results are
quite significantly moderated by such intervention characteristics as the duration of the
gameful experience, the learning environment, design principles, and user type. (Li, Ma, &
Shi, 2023) This creates a direct requirement for the diploma product: to be configurable in
such a way that the same core engine is able to support pedagogically different scenarios,
like supporting intensive drill practice, gap diagnosis or a full mission-based learning
experience with narrative elements. (Li, Ma, & Shi, 2023)
If the focus is narrowed down specifically to intrinsic motivation, the picture appears
more cautious. A meta-analysis of 35 interventions involving approximately 2,500 participants revealed a modest overall impact on intrinsic motivation, along with larger
9

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

impacts on autonomy and relatedness and little impact on competence. (Li, Hew, &
Du, 2024) For AI enhanced game-based learning, this point is very important: AI-based
personalization should not be about entertaining, but rather actively help learners to
build their subjective sense of competence through adaptive challenge levels, diagnostic
feedback and visible progress markers. Otherwise, motivational gains may not be turned
into a real mastery. (Li, Hew, & Du, 2024)
Regarding academic achievement, a meta-analysis of experimental studies between
2008 and 2023 produced a moderately positive effect (Hedges’s gamma of around 0.782)
and emphasized the need for moderators in choosing game elements for various levels of
education and contexts. (Zeng et al., 2024) In other words, gamification has some average
level of effectiveness, but it is not guaranteed to be effective in every particular course
and in every group of learners. This again reinforces the need for data-driven iteration
and context sensitive design adaptation in the proposed application. (Zeng et al., 2024)
The boundaries and dangers of gamification are also borne out by the longitudinal
findings. A study discussing changes in efficacy effects of gamification over time, by
analyzing behavioral outcomes over a semester and discussing potential novelty effects
and the importance of a non-gamified control group to the outcomes of a long-duration
intervention. (Rodrigues et al., 2022) In addition, experimental evidence on personality
traits provide evidence that points, badges and rankings may work differently for different
learners. (Smiderle et al., 2020) For the diploma application this means that personalization should be applied not only at the content level - what is the learner practicing - but
also at the motivational and UX level, such as the way progress is displayed, which types
of social comparison are appropriate, and how public leaderboards should be. (Smiderle
et al., 2020)

2.5

Platform Modes and Functional Patterns: Kahoot!
and Quizizz as Base Line

Empirical studies of popular quiz-based platforms reveal that even fairly simple forms
of games, such as quizzes with immediate feedback, can be linked to positive perceptions and/or improvements in selected cognitive and educational indicators provided
the intervention is of sufficient length and properly embedded in the learning process.
Experimental work comparing groups who were exposed to a quiz platform for varying
lengths of time found improvements in attention, critical thinking and related skills, whilst

2.6

AI-Enhanced Game-Based Learning: Adaptivity,
Personalization, and Stealth Assessment

Moving from gamified practice to an AI-enhanced educational game requires a strong
foundation in research on adaptive game-based learning. A systematic review of adaptive
game-based learning shows that tailoring content and/or learning experience to the
learner is usually associated with better retention and a more meaningful experience.
It also demonstrates the diversity of adaptive approaches: adaptation may affect the
11

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW

selection and presentation of content, the quantity and type of exercises or levels, or
the difficulty adjustment based on performance and engagement data. (Chiotaki et al.,
2023) At the same time, the review notes a common limitation: in many cases, adaptivity
is implemented as a choice among a finite set of pre-authored content packages, which
restricts the depth of personalization and often requires grouping learners by metrics.
(Chiotaki et al., 2023)
At the broader level of AI-adaptive systems, a systematic mapping study of 147 publications from 2014 to 2020 underscores the central idea of data-driven personalization while
also observing that genuinely implemented systems are still relatively few. It therefore
formulates the need for more precise matching between the type of AI intervention and
the specific educational problem being addressed - in effect, design-to-problem matching.
(Kabudi et al., 2021) For the thesis, this means that AI should be operationalized through
clearly defined application functions and testable hypotheses: (1) adaptive difficulty to
keep learners in an optimal challenge zone, (2) recommendation of tasks or missions
based on mistakes and solution time, (3) personalized feedback, and (4) prediction of
learner drop-off risk together with corresponding teacher support signals. (Kabudi et al.,
2021)
A key component of AI-enhanced game-based learning is in-game assessment, or stealth
assessment, in which fine-grained interaction traces are used to infer competence. A study
published in IEEE Transactions on Learning Technologies describes the DEEPSTEALTH
framework, which uses deep neural models to infer competence from sequences of actions
and demonstrates advantages in both predictive accuracy and early detection, while also
reducing the burden of manual feature engineering. (Min et al., 2019/2020) At the same
time, the authors emphasize an important limitation: deep models require further work
on interpretability and explainability, which is directly connected to trustworthiness and
fairness in educational decision-making. (Min et al., 2019/2020)
Finally, the AI layer inevitably raises ethical and privacy issues. International guidance
on generative AI in education emphasizes that the release of new tools is outpacing
national regulatory frameworks and that, in many countries, the lack of clear regulation
leaves users’ personal data insufficiently protected. The guidance proposes policy measures including privacy protection requirements, age restrictions, and a human-centered
approach to ethical validation and pedagogical design. (UNESCO, 2023) In the context
of the diploma application, this becomes a set of non-functional requirements: data
minimization, transparency, consent, access control, the ability to explain why the system
generated a given recommendation, and procedures for monitoring risks such as bias,

3.2

Conceptual Framework

Based on the reviewed literature and benchmark systems, the proposed application should
be guided by a conceptual framework that combines four interdependent components:
(1) learner modelling, (2) adaptive game mechanics, (3) AI-assisted content and feedback
generation, and (4) teacher oversight.
This framework reflects the literature on adaptive game-based learning, which shows
that personalization and adaptation are central design priorities in educational games
(Chiotaki et al., 2023). It also reflects broader evidence that intelligent tutoring systems
tend to be most effective when feedback, task selection, and support are closely matched
to learner needs (Huang et al., 2025). At the same time, teacher perspectives must remain
central, because successful adoption depends on usability, time demands, and institutional
support (Simon & Zeng, 2024).

3.3

How the Proposed Project Improves on Existing
Systems

The proposed project improves on existing systems in three important ways. First, unlike
tools that mainly assist with content or quiz generation, the new application is designed
around an adaptive learner-centred loop in which performance influences subsequent
tasks, difficulty level, and feedback. Second, unlike Duolingo Max, which is highly effective
but domain-specific, the proposed application is intended to support broader educational
contexts rather than only language learning. Third, unlike early research prototypes,
the project explicitly balances AI capability with teacher control, explainable feedback,
and alignment with curricular objectives. As a result, the application aims to move
beyond isolated AI features toward a more complete and classroom-relevant AI-enhanced
game-based learning system.

3.4

Methods and Sources Used to Collect Data for the
Project

Data for the project should be collected through a mixed-source approach. First, a
desk-based review of open-access journal articles, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses
should be used to identify best practices in adaptive learning, game-based learning,
and intelligent tutoring. Second, a comparative review of existing systems should be
conducted using official product documentation, feature pages, and public descriptions
for tools such as Kahoot!, Wayground AI, and Duolingo Max. Third, user requirement
data should be gathered from the target audience through questionnaires, interviews,
or focus groups with students and teachers. Finally, after prototype development, pilot
usability testing and system log data should be used to evaluate engagement, completion,
user satisfaction, and the quality of adaptive responses. This multi-source strategy is
appropriate because both the technical and human dimensions of adoption are critical in
adaptive educational technologies (Simon & Zeng, 2024).

3.5

Challenges and Limitations in Obtaining Relevant
Data

Several challenges may affect data collection for the project. A first limitation is the
cold-start problem: because the system is new, there may be insufficient learner data
at the beginning to support robust personalization. A second challenge is access to
school-based participants, since work with students often requires ethics approval, institutional permission, and informed consent procedures. A third challenge concerns
teacher participation, because adaptive systems may be perceived as time-consuming or
technically demanding. Finally, AI-generated educational content cannot be accepted
without validation. Commercial platforms themselves acknowledge that generated outputs
may contain inaccuracies, which means that quality assurance through teacher or expert
review is likely to be necessary during early development and testing phases (Wayground,
2025a).

This chapter presents the revised methodology and findings based on 100 survey responses.
Survey instrument: online questionnaire administered through Google Forms.
Dataset summary: 100 respondents completed the closed-ended survey; the first

4.1

Introduction

Gamified learning platforms integrate elements such as points, badges, levels, leaderboards,
timers, and immediate feedback into learning activities. These tools are increasingly
common in both school and university settings because they can make routine practice
feel more interactive, competitive, and enjoyable. Platforms such as Kahoot!, Quizizz,
and Quizlet are often used to increase attention, participation, and short-cycle practice
during lessons.
The underlying idea of gamification is not simply to entertain students, but to
align game mechanics with educational goals. When designed well, these mechanics can
encourage repetition, persistence, and quick correction of mistakes. At the same time,
research and classroom experience suggest that gamification is most effective when it
supports learning rather than replacing it. Competition, time pressure, and rewards can
motivate some students, but they may also distract others if the activity becomes too
focused on winning rather than understanding.

4.2

Purpose and Research Focus

The purpose of this updated study was to examine how students perceive gamified
learning after the survey dataset expanded to 100 respondents. The revised version
updates the participant profile, descriptive statistics, and qualitative analysis so that the
methodology and results reflect the larger sample rather than the earlier smaller dataset.
The study addressed two main questions:
• Does the use of gamified platforms increase student engagement and participation?
• Do students believe these platforms improve their learning, memory, and assessment
performance?
By combining quantitative survey results with open-ended responses, the report aims to
identify which gamification elements students value most, which elements create tension
or mixed reactions, and what changes students would make to improve classroom use.

4.3

Methodology

4.3.1

Survey Design

A cross-sectional survey was used to collect student perceptions of gamified learning.
The questionnaire included four parts: (a) demographic information, (b) exposure to
gamified platforms and common features, (c) Likert-scale statements on engagement and
perceived learning outcomes, and (d) open-ended questions asking students to describe
helpful features, concrete learning experiences, and suggested improvements.
Each attitudinal statement used a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly
disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). The updated report keeps the same instrument logic as
the earlier draft, but all narrative interpretation has been recalculated for the expanded
dataset of 100 respondents.

4.3.2

Participants

A total of 100 respondents completed the closed-ended portion of the survey. The sample
was dominated by bachelor-level students and concentrated in the typical university age
range, especially 18-20 and 20-22 years old. Participation was voluntary and anonymous,
allowing students to answer freely about both positive and negative aspects of gamified
learning.

CHAPTER 4. METHODOLOGY AND UPDATED SURVEY ANALYSIS

Open-ended participation varied slightly by question. The first prompt on helpful
features received 100 responses, the second prompt on memorable learning moments also
received 100 responses, and the final prompt on suggested changes received 81 responses.
This means the qualitative section combines full-response and partial-response items.

4.3.3

Data Collection

The survey was distributed online through Google Forms. Responses were collected in
a format that automatically generated summary charts for the closed-ended questions.
These updated Google Forms screenshots were used as the visual basis for the figures
embedded in this report. Because the instrument was completed online, students could
respond conveniently and with a degree of privacy that may improve candor in self-report
data.

4.3.4

Data Analysis

Quantitative data were analyzed using descriptive statistics, primarily frequencies and
percentages. The interpretation focuses on overall patterns, such as the proportion of
positive responses (Agree + Strongly Agree), neutral responses, and negative responses
(Disagree + Strongly Disagree). No inferential statistics were applied because the goal of
the study was descriptive rather than causal.
Qualitative responses were reviewed using a light thematic approach. Recurring
patterns were grouped into broad themes such as immediate feedback, progress/rewards,
competition, repetition/retries, and suggestions for classroom improvement. To keep the
report readable, the qualitative appendix presents a condensed thematic coding table
with representative excerpts rather than reproducing every raw screenshot page.

Discussion

The updated dataset strengthens the original interpretation rather than reversing it.
Across 100 respondents, gamification appears to be a broadly positive support for
attention, participation, and short-term learning confidence. Students clearly associate
gamified activities with higher energy, more active recall, and better awareness of mistakes.
At the same time, the larger sample also makes the limits of gamification more visible.
Not every student experiences competition in the same way, and speed-based design
remains the clearest source of mixed reactions. The results therefore support a balanced
model: gamification works best when it combines motivation with explanatory feedback,
retries, and meaningful practice rather than relying only on ranking and time pressure.
Another important finding is that students are comfortable with multiple delivery
27

CHAPTER 4. METHODOLOGY AND UPDATED SURVEY ANALYSIS

formats. Self-paced mode and live whole-class mode both received strong support, which
suggests that teachers do not need a single rigid template. Instead, they can adapt the
format to the lesson goal—for example, fast group reviews before an exam, or slower
self-paced practice for consolidation and homework.

4.6

Limitations

This study has several limitations. First, it relies on self-reported perception rather than
direct performance data, so students’ impressions of improved learning may not always
match measured achievement. Second, the sample is a convenience sample rather than
a random one, which limits generalizability. Third, the results largely reflect university
students—especially bachelor-level respondents—so they should be extended to other
populations with caution.
In addition, the open-ended responses were analyzed from Google Forms screenshots
rather than from a fully exported spreadsheet. For that reason, the qualitative coding
should be read as descriptive and illustrative rather than as a line-by-line exhaustive
coding of every response.

4.7

Summary

The revised survey analysis based on 100 responses confirms that gamified learning is
widely viewed as beneficial for student engagement. Most respondents reported paying
more attention, participating more actively, and remembering content better when
gamified tools were used.
However, the findings also make clear that not all game mechanics are equally effective.
Immediate feedback, retries, progress tracking, points, and flexible pacing were the most
consistently valued features. By contrast, timers and speed-focused competition produced
more divided reactions.
Overall, the updated methodology and results suggest that gamification is most
effective when it is used as a structured learning aid rather than as entertainment alone.
Students respond best when the activity helps them practice, understand mistakes quickly,
track improvement, and reflect on answers after the game. For teachers, the implication is
straightforward: design gamified activities around learning goals first, and use competition
only when it supports—not replaces—understanding.

6.3

Frontend Technology: Flutter

For the client layer, the most relevant alternatives were Flutter, React Native, and
native Android development. Native Android can offer strong device-level control and
platform-specific optimization, but it would narrow the MVP to one operating system
and increase implementation effort. React Native is also a cross-platform option, but
it depends more heavily on the JavaScript ecosystem and typically requires additional
integration effort for highly consistent UI behavior across screens.
Flutter was selected because it fits the mobile-first nature of the project while allowing
the team to build one coherent interface in a single codebase. This is especially useful for
a gamified educational platform, where visual consistency, smooth navigation, animated
progress indicators, and reusable UI components are important. Flutter also matches
the project plan, where frontend work focuses on core screens, user flows, gamification
UI, and the AI assistant interface. In practical terms, Flutter offers a good balance
between development speed and interface quality, making it more suitable than fully
native development for a semester-bounded MVP.

6.4

Backend and API Layer: Python with FastAPI

At the backend level, the main alternatives were FastAPI, Django REST Framework, and
a Node.js-based API framework such as Express or NestJS. Django REST Framework is
mature and feature-rich, but it is comparatively heavier for a project whose main need is
a clean API layer and orchestration of separate services. A Node.js stack is also viable,
especially for event-driven systems, but it would split the technology focus between
Python-oriented AI work and JavaScript-oriented backend work.
FastAPI was chosen because it is lightweight, well-suited to REST API development,
and fits naturally with Python-based AI integration. In this project, the API Gateway/BFF is responsible for request validation, orchestration of internal service calls, and
versioned client-facing APIs. FastAPI supports this role well, while keeping the backend
simple enough for rapid development and testing. Its compatibility with pytest further
supports maintainability and automated validation. The selection therefore improves
developer productivity and reduces friction between backend development and AI-related
modules.

6.5

Database and Authentication: Firebase / Firestore
/ Firebase Auth

For persistent data and identity management, three broad options were considered:
Firebase with Firestore and Firebase Authentication, a traditional relational database
such as PostgreSQL with custom authentication, and a managed backend platform such
as Supabase. A PostgreSQL-based design provides strong relational guarantees and
advanced querying, but it also increases setup and operational complexity, especially
when combined with custom authentication and role handling. Supabase is attractive as
a managed platform, but in the current project materials Firebase is already treated as
the trusted identity provider and primary database, which aligns more directly with the
chosen architecture.
Firebase was selected because it simplifies MVP delivery while still supporting the
project’s main requirements. Firebase Authentication provides token-based identity flows,
while Firestore can support structured educational data, progress records, and servicespecific adapters. This choice also fits the architecture package, where IAM verifies
Firebase tokens and where Firestore is accessed through repository adapters rather than
being exposed directly to all services. The result is a stack that is easier to implement
43

CHAPTER 6. TECHNOLOGY COMPARISON AND TECHNOLOGY USED

quickly than a fully self-managed relational setup, while still leaving room for later
abstraction and migration if needed.

6.6

AI Integration and Orchestration: LLM API with
n8n

The AI layer is one of the most distinctive parts of the project, so several integration
patterns were possible. The simplest option would be to call an LLM API directly from
the backend. Another option would be to adopt a heavier orchestration framework and
embed all prompt routing and workflow logic directly in application code. The project
instead selected a hybrid model: a dedicated AI Tutor Gateway in the backend, combined
with n8n for workflow orchestration and an external LLM API for generation.
This choice is justified by both system quality requirements and educational safety
needs. The architecture documents emphasize that only the AI Tutor Gateway should
communicate with external AI systems, and that AI help must support safety filters,
rate limits, caching, logging, and fallback behavior. n8n is useful in this context because
it externalizes prompt flows and routing logic, making the AI pipeline easier to iterate on
without tightly coupling every change to core backend code. The final arrangement - AI
Tutor Gateway plus n8n plus LLM API - provides enough flexibility for hint generation,
explanation flows, and adaptive feedback while keeping the rest of the system protected
from direct model dependency.

6.7

File Storage: S3/MinIO

The platform needs separate handling for media assets and learning materials, such as
uploaded files, images, or attachments. The main alternatives were S3/MinIO, Firebase
Storage, and local server storage. Local storage is the least suitable option because it is
not robust for deployment, scaling, or multi-service access patterns. Firebase Storage
would integrate well with the Firebase ecosystem, but the architecture package already
assumes storage abstraction through a dedicated content/storage adapter rather than
tightly coupling all files to a single managed backend vendor.
S3/MinIO was selected because it fits that adapter-based architecture and cleanly
separates structured data from file storage. In practice, metadata can remain in the
content domain, while large files are stored in an object store designed for that purpose.

This improves maintainability, reduces schema pollution in the main database, and offers
flexibility between cloud and self-hosted deployment models. For an academic MVP,
this is a pragmatic middle path: simple enough to use, but architecturally cleaner than
keeping all binary assets inside the core database layer.

Testing, Version Control, and Deployment
Support

The support toolchain is also part of the technology decision. The project presentation
identifies Git and Docker as core engineering tools, with pytest for backend testing and
Flutter tests for UI and integration checks. Alternative approaches such as purely manual
testing or unmanaged environment setup would be faster in the short term, but they
would reduce reproducibility and make integration problems harder to detect.
The selected toolchain strengthens the overall quality of the project. Git supports
collaboration, rollback, and branch-based feature development. Docker improves environment consistency across developer machines and future deployment targets. pytest
is appropriate for validating backend APIs, scoring logic, and internal service behavior,
while Flutter tests are suitable for checking UI flows and client-side correctness. Together,
these tools increase reliability without adding disproportionate complexity, which makes
them highly appropriate for a student team delivering a working MVP.

Rationale for the Final Technology Stack

The final technology stack is coherent because each selected tool supports a specific
architectural responsibility instead of duplicating the role of another layer. Flutter
addresses the mobile user experience; FastAPI supports the public API and service

stores larger learning materials; n8n and the LLM API enable flexible AI workflows; and

This separation of concerns is important for a project that combines standard learning
workflows with AI-enhanced behavior.
Another reason for these choices is that they align with the project architecture. The
Mobile App communicates only with the API Gateway/BFF. Quiz submission remains a
protected core flow, while gamification and analytics are updated asynchronously. The

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This diploma project demonstrates that an AI-enhanced game-based learning application
can be justified both pedagogically and technically. The literature review shows that
game-based and gamified environments are most effective when they combine meaningful
challenge, immediate feedback, repetition, and learner-centred design rather than relying
on speed or competition alone. The updated survey with 100 responses reinforces this
conclusion by showing that students value retries, progress visibility, explanations, and
balanced challenge more consistently than public ranking or strict time pressure.
On the basis of these findings, the thesis proposes a coherent minimum viable product,
a structured domain model, and a service-oriented architecture that separate learning
content, quiz logic, gamification, analytics, and AI assistance. The selected technology
stack is appropriate for an academic prototype because it supports mobile delivery,
modular backend logic, controlled AI integration, and future scalability.
Overall, the project provides a research-grounded blueprint for a personalized educational platform in which AI functions as a support layer for adaptation, feedback, and
analytics, while game-based interaction creates a more engaging and responsive learning
experience. Future work should focus on prototype implementation, classroom testing,
longitudinal evaluation, and further refinement of privacy, safety, and explainability
mechanisms.
